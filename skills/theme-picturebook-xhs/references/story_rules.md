# Story Rules

## Theme Interpretation

Turn the user's theme into a picturebook premise with four parts:

- `theme`: the literal topic, such as Spring Festival, rabbit, lotus, snow, courage, or forest.
- `child-facing hook`: what a child can see, touch, wonder about, or play with.
- `parent-facing value`: companionship, emotional education, nature observation, habit building, festival knowledge, or language development.
- `visual hook`: the image motif that should appear on the cover and recur inside the story.

For festival themes, include one concrete festival object or ritual. For animal themes, make the animal behavior part of the plot. For plant themes, show growth, seasons, care, or observation instead of static facts.

## Multi-Dimensional Educational Reading

Before writing the story, create a compact theme knowledge card. Interpret the same theme from multiple dimensions so repeated use of the same theme can produce different stories.

For plant themes, consider:

- name origin, common aliases, or why the name sounds memorable
- flowering period, fruiting period, or seasonal changes
- flower, leaf, stem, fruit, color, shape, and scent
- growth habit, habitat, sunlight, water, soil, or care needs
- pollinators, birds, insects, or other ecological relationships
- child-observable details, such as smell, petal shape, leaf texture, seed movement, or changes after rain
- cultural associations, daily-life uses, poems, food, festivals, gardens, or family memories

For animal themes, consider habitat, body features, behavior, diet, movement, sounds, lifecycle, relationship with people, and ecological role.

For festival themes, consider origin, ritual, food, objects, family activity, season, color, sound, smell, and child participation.

For emotion or habit themes, consider body feeling, daily scene, trigger, misunderstanding, practice method, repair action, and parent-child conversation.

Select only 2-4 facts for the story. The facts must become plot material, not an encyclopedia paragraph. Good uses include:

- a question the child character asks
- a clue that helps solve the story problem
- a repeated visual motif
- a gentle discovery at the turning point
- a short parent-child reading note in Xiaohongshu copy

If a factual claim is uncertain, verify it from reliable sources when possible. If verification is not available, phrase it cautiously or omit the claim. Do not invent exact flowering months, name origins, medical uses, toxicity claims, or cultural history.

## Audience Adaptation

Default audience is ages 3-8:

- Use simple motivations and visible actions.
- Avoid long exposition, abstract moralizing, and adult conflict.
- Keep tension mild and solvable.
- Use repeated motifs or repeated sentences when helpful.
- End with warmth, discovery, repair, or shared joy.

For younger children, reduce plot complexity and increase repetition. For older children, allow a clearer problem, more causal steps, and light science or cultural knowledge.

## Narrative Quality Gate

Before page allocation, make the story feel like a real children's picturebook rather than a list of scenes. Require:

- a child-scaled wish, question, worry, or promise that starts the story engine
- one gentle obstacle or misunderstanding that matters emotionally but is not frightening
- a clear cause-and-effect chain where pages connect by "because" and "so", not just "then"
- a repeated object, sound, phrase, path, or visual motif that gives rhythm and helps children anticipate
- an emotional arc that changes step by step, such as curious -> unsure -> trying -> disappointed -> noticing -> brave -> relieved
- a turning point prepared by earlier clues, character choices, or repeated motifs
- a climax that lets the child characters actively help solve the problem instead of being rescued by coincidence
- a closing image that echoes the opening but shows what has changed
- no forced moral, coincidence-only solution, unexplained helper, or sudden emotional reversal

Run these checks before writing prompts:

- `because test`: every beat can be explained as "because the previous beat happened, this happens now"
- `swap test`: neighboring pages cannot be swapped without damaging the story
- `turning-point setup test`: the turning point has at least one earlier visual or emotional setup
- `child-understanding test`: a child can answer "what do they want?", "what is hard?", and "what changed?"
- `not-contrived test`: the problem, attempts, turning point, and solution are all natural consequences of the characters' earlier choices, observations, promises, mistakes, or theme facts. If a beat only works because the author needs the lesson to happen, rewrite it.

## Fixed IP Mode

Children's picturebooks must use the fixed long-term IP characters from `ip_bible.md`. Do not create a new lead cast for each theme.

For each story:

- choose 2-3 recurring characters as the main cast
- keep all appearance anchors unchanged
- add only temporary theme-specific props, costumes, or side characters
- remove temporary props at the end unless the user asks to update the IP
- never change species, body colors, signature clothing, or core personality

## Character Lock Sheet

Create an episode lock sheet from the IP bible before image prompts. For each main character include:

- name
- role in story
- type/species
- apparent age or age feeling
- height and silhouette
- face features
- main colors
- clothing or signature prop
- personality
- expression range
- theme-specific prop allowed in this episode
- forbidden drift

Use 2-3 recurring characters by default. Use 4 only when the story genuinely needs it. Avoid adding new named characters after the lock sheet unless necessary.

## Story Structure

Write the complete story first, then split it into pages. A complete story must include:

1. opening situation
2. inciting discovery or event
3. question, wish, or small problem
4. first attempt
5. second attempt
6. obstacle or misunderstanding
7. turning point
8. cooperative action
9. climax or reveal
10. resolution
11. gentle takeaway
12. closing image

The story should be able to stand alone as text before image prompts are written.

Light educational content should be woven into the story arc. Do not place all facts on one page, and do not let facts interrupt the emotional flow.

Do not make the turning point a sudden discovery, coincidence, new helper, or unexplained fact. Prepare it through a clue, failed attempt, remembered advice, sensory detail, repeated phrase, child decision, or theme fact that appeared earlier. The turn should feel inevitable in hindsight: a parent should be able to point to an earlier page and say, "This is why the ending works."

Do not flatten the middle into repeated discoveries with the same emotional value. Each attempt should change at least one thing: the character's plan, feeling, knowledge, relationship, location, or risk.

Avoid contrived story logic. Do not force characters to make obviously unreasonable choices just to create a lesson, do not solve the problem with a convenient new object or adult rescue, and do not jump from worry to confidence without a visible practice, discovery, repair, or shared action.

Use a recurring phrase, sound, visual object, or small ritual when it improves read-aloud rhythm. Vary it slightly as the story develops so children feel both familiarity and progress.

## Story-To-Page Allocation

After writing the complete story, create a story-beat allocation plan before drafting page text. Do not brainstorm 12 isolated page ideas.

For each beat, define:

- `story event`: the event from the complete story
- `cause from previous beat`: why this beat happens now
- `visible action`: what the illustration can show
- `visual distinctness`: how this beat looks different from the previous beat in setting, camera distance, character pose, object state, or emotional focus
- `emotional shift`: how the character's feeling or understanding changes
- `next-page hook`: what question, action, object, or discovery carries the reader into the next page

Only after this allocation is clear, write the page-by-page storyboard.

Before finalizing page count, run a visual-overlap pass. If two adjacent beats would show nearly the same setting, camera angle, character pose, object arrangement, and emotional value, merge them into one stronger page instead of producing two near-duplicate images. The merged page must still preserve the causal sequence in its page text and prompt. If merging lowers the count, add pages only for real missing story beats or needed transitions; do not split duplicate visuals just to inflate the count.

Choose page count after the allocation plan:

- Use 12 interior pages only for simple one-location stories with one clear problem and a quick resolution.
- Use 14 or 16 interior pages for festival preparation, plant growth, nature observation, a small journey, or stories with two meaningful attempts before the turn.
- Use 18 or 20 interior pages when the story needs a gentler setup, more child-readable transitions, a multi-stage process, or a fuller emotional repair.
- Expand beyond 20 only if the user asks or the theme truly requires it; keep the final sequence practical for Xiaohongshu.

Never cut a needed transition just to keep 12 pages. Add pages only for real story beats, not static facts or decorative scenes.

## Storyboard Rules

Default to at least 12 interior pages, with the exact count determined by story rhythm:

- Page 1 establishes the place, main character, and theme object.
- Pages 2-3 introduce the question or goal.
- Pages 4-8 show attempts, discovery, or process.
- Pages 9-10 deliver the turning point and climax.
- Page 11 resolves the emotional or practical problem.
- The final page gives a warm closing image suitable for the last Xiaohongshu image.

Use 12 pages for simple themes. Use 14, 16, 18, or 20 pages when the theme needs more steps, such as a festival preparation process, plant growth cycle, journey, multi-stage nature observation, or a softer emotional turn. Do not expand pages just to add static knowledge cards.

Each page must include:

- page number
- story beat
- page text
- image action
- characters visible
- previous-page link
- next-page hook
- continuity note

Page text is mandatory for every interior page and must be written so readers understand the exact page content, action change, emotional shift, or story transition. It should accurately describe what is happening in the current illustration, not merely name the theme or repeat a vague caption. Prefer two to three short child-readable sentences, or three to four short lines, when the picture needs context; one sentence is acceptable only when the image is extremely simple and already clear. Do not write a dense paragraph. The text should sound read-aloud friendly, with concrete actions, sensory details, clear cause-and-effect, and gentle rhythm. This page text must appear inside the final interior image, placed in quiet visual areas, with uniform typography: every character in the same sentence uses the same size, weight, style, and baseline. If one page combines two visually overlapping beats, include two separate sentences, place them in staggered quiet areas such as upper-left sky and lower-left floor, and make each sentence describe its own beat. Each interior page must also show its Arabic page number in the lower-right corner, starting from 1 and continuing sequentially; the cover has no page number. Avoid using captions as a substitute for action.

Adjacent pages must be connected by cause, discovery, repeated object, character decision, emotional change, or movement through the setting. If two neighboring pages can be swapped without changing the story, rewrite them because they are too independent.

## Safety Handling

Transform risky themes instead of refusing when a safe adaptation is possible:

- disaster -> preparation, helping, weather observation, rebuilding
- death or extinction -> memory, care, lifecycle, protection
- disease -> rest, hygiene, care, recovery
- fear -> curiosity, night sounds, companionship
- punishment -> consequence, repair, apology
- competition -> effort, cooperation, celebrating differences

Do not include graphic harm, humiliation, cruelty, or frightening imagery for the default child audience.
